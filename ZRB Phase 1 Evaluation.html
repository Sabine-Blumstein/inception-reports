Section 1

Evaluation Purpose and Scope

This inception report outlines the evaluation team's proposed approach, methodology, and work plan for the external evaluation of the Climate Resilient Integrated Water Resources Management in Zarafshan River Basin (ZRB) Project Phase 1 (2023–2027), commissioned by the Embassy of Switzerland in Uzbekistan.

Dual Purpose of the Evaluation

The evaluation serves two interrelated purposes. First, it provides an independent assessment of Phase 1 performance against the six OECD/DAC criteria — Relevance, Coherence, Effectiveness, Efficiency, Impact, and Sustainability — drawing on available evidence from project documents, monitoring data, and stakeholder interviews. Second, it is explicitly forward-looking: findings and recommendations are intended to inform the design of Phase 2, including decisions on implementation arrangements, consortium composition, and strategic focus. While this evaluation will generate concrete recommendations for Phase 2, its primary output is an independent assessment and a set of design parameters and options for the next phase.

Per the Terms of Reference (ToR), the evaluation places primary emphasis on institutional sustainability and systemic impact — specifically, whether technical innovations and governance structures supported by the project are sufficiently embedded within national institutions to survive the project's eventual exit. Alongside this, the evaluation will assess the depth and quality of inclusive participation, examining the extent to which women, Water Consumer Associations (WCAs), and downstream users have meaningfully engaged in basin-level water governance.

Geographic Scope

Zarafshan River Basin in Uzbekistan, including pilot areas in the Samarkand and Kashkadarya regions and relevant national-level institutions in Tashkent.

Temporal Scope

Phase 1: June 2023 to the time of evaluation (mid-2026). Phase 1 is still underway; findings will therefore reflect an interim, mid-phase perspective.

The evaluation is conducted by a two-person team: Sabine Blumstein (international consultant and team leader, Water Connect Consulting) and Shakhboz Akhmedov (local consultant, Tashkent). The team reports to the Head of Cooperation at the Embassy of Switzerland in Tashkent.

Section 2

Project Mandate and Theory of Change

The ZRB project is implemented by a consortium comprising Caritas Switzerland (CACH) as lead implementing agency, the Regional Environmental Centre for Central Asia (CAREC), hydrosolutions GmbH as technical partner, and MeteoSwiss. The project runs from June 2023 to 2027 with SDC funding, and forms part of Switzerland's broader Water and Energy cooperation programme in Uzbekistan, which also includes the National Water Resources Management (NWRM), Uzbekistan Water Services and Institutional Support Program (WASIS), and Blue Peace Central Asia projects.

Project Outcomes

Outcome 1

Climate-resilient IWRM supported by improved data, digital tools, and institutional capacity — through hydrometeorological monitoring systems, the Count4D digital water accounting tool, and strengthened operational capacity of the River Basin Council (RBC) and relevant water management authorities.

Outcome 2

Water users — female and male — benefit from and engage in equitable, integrated water resource management, including through Special Water Service Units strengthening, regenerative agriculture uptake, and gender-balanced participation in basin governance.

Outcome 3

Policymakers institutionalize tools and approaches from the ZRB project, supporting climate-resilient IWRM within Uzbekistan's ongoing multi-sectoral water reforms and contributing to transboundary dialogue with Tajikistan.

Theory of Change

IF water users and managers are equipped with sound evidence, relevant tools, and effective institutions (Outcome 1), AND IF all water users are empowered to participate in and benefit from basin-wide IWRM (Outcome 2), AND IF national policymakers institutionalize these approaches within ongoing multi-sectoral reform processes and transboundary cooperation (Outcome 3), THEN climate-resilient livelihoods, food security, and environmental integrity can be strengthened — because evidence-based, inclusive, and institutionally embedded water governance is an essential pathway to sustainable development.

Section 3

Evaluation Questions

The evaluation covers all six OECD/DAC criteria: Relevance, Coherence, Effectiveness, Efficiency, Impact, and Sustainability. In line with the ToR, primary emphasis is placed on Sustainability and Impact, understood in institutional and systemic terms, and on governance, gender, and social inclusion across all criteria. Relevance, Coherence and Effectiveness are treated as core explanatory criteria, while Efficiency is addressed in a more focused way through an assessment of the consortium set-up and delivery model.

During the inception phase, the evaluation team reviewed, restructured, and refined the indicative evaluation questions provided in the ToR. The revised question set is presented in summary form in the table below; the full set including sub-questions, forward-looking questions, and the evaluation matrix is in Annex 1. The following changes were made relative to the ToR:

  • Structure and numbering: All questions have been renumbered sequentially as EQ-1 through EQ-16. Sub-questions are presented as unnumbered bullet points under each main EQ. Forward-looking questions are excluded from the evaluation matrix and will inform the recommendations sections of the final report.
  • Criterion added: Coherence was added as a standalone criterion section. The coherence question (EQ-3) is therefore new.
  • Questions merged: Several ToR questions were consolidated to reduce overlap and sharpen analytical focus. Upstream/downstream differentiation and RBC institutionalization sub-questions are folded into the relevant main EQs.
  • New questions added: EQ-5 on adaptive management responses to implementation delays; a sub-question under EQ-6 on internal consortium coordination effectiveness — both based on topics raised at the kick-off meeting.
  • Question dropped: The ToR includes an efficiency question on whether the project's integrated approach — combining technological innovation with institutional and governance support — demonstrates cost-effectiveness for basin-level reform. After the desk review, the evaluation team proposes to drop this as a standalone evaluation question. A robust cost-effectiveness assessment would require detailed, comparable financial data and a credible counterfactual (what a non-integrated approach would have cost), which are unlikely to be available within the scope and timing of this mid-phase review. In addition, the substantial implementation delays and institutional disruptions experienced in Phase 1 make it methodologically difficult to disentangle the inherent efficiency of the model from context-driven factors. Instead, qualitative aspects of efficiency and value-for-money — including the suitability of the consortium set-up, transaction costs, and perceived benefits of the integrated approach — will be analysed under EQ-6 using available financial information and stakeholder perspectives.

Forward-Looking Questions

The following recommendation-oriented questions are excluded from the evaluation matrix as they call for synthesis and judgment rather than independent empirical investigation. They will be addressed in the conclusions and recommendations sections of the final report:

What are the main practical recommendations and adjustments to strengthen the relevance of the project's climate-resilient IWRM approach for Phase 2?
What adjustments are needed to improve the efficiency of the project's operational and governance support mechanisms for Phase 2?
What are the recommendations for scaling the project's climate-resilient IWRM and digital water accounting pilots to other sub-basins or nationally?
What are the recommendations for ensuring the long-term financial and institutional sustainability of the RBC and the modernized monitoring network?

Section 4

Approach and Methodology

4.1 Key Principles and Methods

The evaluation will apply a utilisation-focused, participatory, and mixed-methods design, aligned with OECD/DAC evaluation criteria:

  • Utilisation-focused and participatory: The Embassy of Switzerland, SDC, and implementing partners will be engaged at key stages to strengthen ownership, contextual grounding, and practical uptake of findings.
  • Mixed-methods and triangulated: Evidence gathered through structured document review, semi-structured key informant interviews, direct field observation, and participation in the River Basin Council meeting. Findings will be cross-validated across data sources and methods.
  • Institutional and systemic lens: The evaluation will assess the degree to which project-supported innovations — including digital water accounting tools, hydrometeorological systems, and the RBC — are embedded, or on a credible trajectory toward embedding, within national institutions, regulatory frameworks, and public budgetary processes.
  • Governance, gender and inclusion focus: Beyond formal participation metrics, the evaluation will examine the quality and depth of stakeholder engagement, including the meaningful involvement of women, WCAs, and downstream users in water governance processes.
  • Forward-looking: The evaluation will generate actionable recommendations oriented toward Phase 2 design, including on implementation arrangements, consortium structure, institutional partnerships, and thematic priorities.
  • Do no harm: Ethical safeguards will be applied throughout. Participants will be offered the option of anonymity and informed of the evaluation's independence.

The evaluation will apply a contribution analysis approach to assess the plausibility of the project's ToC. Given the mid-phase timing, full attribution of impact is neither expected nor claimed.

4.3 Interviews

Semi-structured key informant interviews are the primary source of qualitative primary data. Interviews are organised in two phases:

  • Online pre-mission interviews (April–early May 2026): Targeting stakeholders accessible remotely, including some staff from Caritas Switzerland headquarters, Embassy of Switzerland members, Swiss technical solution providers (hydrosolutions GmbH, MeteoSwiss), and selected previous project team leaders.
  • In-country interviews and field mission (week of 18 May 2026): Anchored to the River Basin Council meeting (20/21 May 2026), with bilateral interviews and group discussions in Tashkent and in the ZRB pilot regions (Samarkand and Kashkadarya). The detailed in-country schedule is being finalised by the local consultant.

Interview targets include: Embassy of Switzerland in Tashkent; Caritas Switzerland (CACH) headquarters and in-country team; CAREC programme and technical staff; hydrosolutions GmbH and MeteoSwiss; Ministry of Water Resources; National Committee on Ecology and Climate Change; Ministry of Agriculture; Ministry of Geology and Mining; UzHydroMet; RBC members and secretariat; BISA staff; WCA representatives (including female members and leaders); farmers and beneficiaries in pilot areas (upstream and downstream); representatives of other Swiss water interventions; and other development partners (EU, World Bank, as relevant).

Interviews will be conducted in English, Uzbek, and possibly Russian as appropriate. Shakhboz Akhmedov will provide interpretation and translation support. All interviews will be documented through detailed notes; audio recording only with explicit participant consent. A structured stakeholder list (50+ entries) has been prepared and shared with implementing partners for contact detail population.

4.4 Synthesis and Reporting

Findings will be synthesised around each OECD/DAC criterion and main evaluation question following a finding → evidence → conclusion → recommendation logic. Triangulation will be applied systematically. Where evidence is limited or conflicting, this will be explicitly flagged, and the evaluation will distinguish between findings directly supported by reviewed materials, logical inferences, and gaps where evidence is insufficient.

The draft evaluation report (target: 12 June 2026) will be shared with the Embassy for review and comment. A debriefing session will be held at the Embassy of Switzerland in Tashkent at the close of the field mission. The final report (due 30 June 2026) will incorporate Embassy feedback and include the completed SDC Assessment Grid for the DAC Criteria as an annex.

4.5 Evaluation Team

International Consultant / Team Leader

Sabine Blumstein, Water Connect Consulting. Leads evaluation design, manages client relations, conducts interviews, and is responsible for all report drafting, editing, and quality assurance.

Local Consultant

Shakhboz Akhmedov, Tashkent. Leads field logistics, stakeholder coordination, interpretation and translation, and provides contextual analysis of the Uzbek water sector and institutional landscape.

4.6 Limitations

Mid-phase timingThe evaluation is conducted before Phase 1 is complete. Impact-level results cannot be fully assessed at this stage; the evaluation will focus on output and outcome-level evidence and assess the trajectory toward longer-term results.
AttributionThe ZRB project operates in a complex multi-actor environment involving multiple ministries, donors, and international organisations. Attributing changes in water governance solely to the project is not feasible; contribution analysis will be applied.
Document gapsSeveral key documents — including the logframe with indicator tracking data and RBC governance records — are outstanding at the time of drafting. Where these remain unavailable at the time of the field mission, findings in affected areas will be flagged as provisional.
Access and sensitivitySome interview topics — particularly questions regarding the authenticity of project activities and consortium dynamics — may be politically or reputationally sensitive. The evaluation team will manage these with high discretion and rely on triangulation across multiple sources.
Language and translationMost field interviews will require interpretation. Interview notes will flag where interpretation constraints may have affected evidence quality.

5.2 Detailed Field Mission Plan — Week of 18 May 2026

The in-country mission will take place in the week of 18 May 2026 and is structured around three objectives: (i) observing the functioning of the Zarafshan River Basin Council and related governance processes; (ii) triangulating project results and institutional changes through interviews with national stakeholders in Tashkent; and (iii) validating field-level changes, including digital tools and agricultural practices, with water users and local authorities in the Zarafshan River Basin. The precise schedule and other mission details will be adjusted as needed based on stakeholder availability and logistical considerations.

1
Day 1 — Monday, 18 May 2026
Arrival and Tashkent consultations
  • Arrival of the international consultant in Tashkent; internal briefing with the local consultant.
  • Courtesy meeting and briefing with the Embassy of Switzerland (Head of Cooperation, Portfolio Manager, NPO).
  • Meetings with Caritas Switzerland and CAREC project management teams (project strategy, implementation progress, major challenges, expectations for Phase 2).
  • Evening team debrief to refine key lines of inquiry for subsequent days.
2
Day 2 — Tuesday, 19 May 2026
National-level institutions and RBC preparation
  • Meetings with Ministry of Water Resources (MinWR) and relevant departments (basin management, data and planning) on institutional reforms, RBC mandate, and adoption of project innovations.
  • Meeting with UzHydroMet on hydrometeorological monitoring network, data integration, and sustainability of equipment and platforms.
  • Meeting with Ministry of Ecology and Climate Change / National Committee on Ecology and Climate Change and, if feasible, Ministry of Agriculture and Ministry of Geology and Mining on policy coherence and cross-sectoral integration.
  • Short preparatory exchange with the RBC secretariat and implementing partners regarding the upcoming council meeting (agenda, composition, key decisions to be taken).
3
Day 3 — Wednesday, 20 May 2026
RBC meeting observation — Kashkadarya region
  • Travel to Kashkadarya region (pilot area).
  • Participation as observers in the Zarafshan River Basin Council meeting.
  • During breaks and immediately after the session, short semi-structured conversations with a cross-section of RBC members (upstream/downstream, women and men, different stakeholder categories) on their roles, perceived influence, and views on the project.
  • Debrief between evaluators to document key observations on governance, participation, decision-making dynamics, and data use in the RBC.
4
Day 4 — Thursday, 21 May 2026
Samarkand region field visits
  • Travel to Samarkand region (pilot area).
  • Site visits to project-supported hydro-met stations, Count4D / digital water accounting applications, and Special Water Service Units (as applicable) with local MinWR/BISA staff.
  • Group discussions and/or focus groups with Water Consumer Associations and farmers (including women farmers and tail-end users) on irrigation reliability, access to information, and participation in water governance.
  • Short meeting with local authorities (e.g. hokimiyat or sectoral departments) on the perceived benefits and challenges of the ZRB project.
5
Day 5 — Friday, 22 May 2026
Concluding meetings and Embassy debriefing
  • Return travel to Tashkent in the evening.
  • Internal synthesis meeting between the international and local consultants to consolidate field notes, identify emerging patterns per evaluation question, and note evidence gaps.
  • Debriefing meeting at the Embassy of Switzerland to present preliminary findings, test emerging conclusions, and discuss implications for Phase 2 design.

EQ-7
Does the project's integrated approach — combining technological innovation with institutional and governance support — demonstrate cost-effectiveness for basin-level reform?
★
▼
Proposed for removal. Assessing cost-effectiveness in this sense would require detailed financial data and a credible counterfactual — what a non-integrated approach would have cost — unlikely to be available or verifiable within this evaluation's scope. Given the significant implementation delays during Phase 1, disentangling efficiency gains or losses attributable to the model itself from those caused by the disruptions of the first phase would also be methodologically very difficult. The evaluation team recommends focusing the efficiency criterion on EQ-6, which addresses the consortium setup and delivery model in terms that are more tractable with available evidence.
